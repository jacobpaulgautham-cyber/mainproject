THE CRAFT

Five chapters. One cloth. A hundred years of knowledge in every thread.

I — Thread II — Loom III — Weave IV — Pattern V — Cloth
I

Chapter I

THE THREAD

Every cloth begins not at the loom, but much earlier, at the spinning wheel. Raw cotton arrives at the cooperative, cleaned and combed into rovings. The spinner, often a woman of the household, draws the fibre onto the bobbin with practiced rhythm. The weight of the thread, the tension of her arm, these are things no machine can replicate.

"The yarn holds the memory of the hand that spun it."

At Eravathody, yarns are sourced from approved suppliers holding the Handloom Mark certification, ensuring that the fibre quality reflects the same standard as the weave.

II

Chapter II

THE LOOM

Eravathody's 19 looms are pit looms, traditional frame looms where the weaver sits level with the cloth, legs hanging into a pit below. Setting up the loom is a full day's labour before a single thread of the weft is passed. The warp threads, numbering in the hundreds, must be individually threaded through the heddles and reed.

"To dress a loom is to make a decision that lasts months."

The tension of each warp thread is calibrated by hand and eye. A weaver at Eravathody works from memory and instinct, there is no digital readout, only the sound of the shed opening cleanly.

III

Chapter III

THE WEAVE

The act of weaving at Eravathody is one of precise repetition, and infinite variation. The shuttle carrying the weft thread passes through the shed, the beater presses each pick into place, the treadle shifts the harness. Each cycle takes about two seconds. A full saree takes days.

"Every throw of the shuttle is a decision made in the body, not the mind."

The rhythm of the loom is audible from outside the weaving shed. It is not mechanical noise, it is the sound of a craft being practised with the whole body.

IV

Chapter IV

THE PATTERN

The Kasavu border, a band of zari (golden thread) running along the edge of Kerala's traditional cloth, is Eravathody's signature. It requires a second shuttle carrying gold-wrapped thread, worked in alongside the plain weft in a precisely timed sequence.

"The Theyyam motif enters the cloth like a prayer, repeated until it becomes invisible."

Artisan Mr. Sundaram Muda's contribution was to bring the visual language of Theyyam, the ritual art form of North Kerala, into the saree's body pattern. The result earned the Society district-level recognition and continues to be one of its most distinctive offerings.

V

Chapter V

THE CLOTH

The finished cloth is cut from the loom and taken through finishing, washing, starching lightly, and drying in open air. At Eravathody, sarees are folded in the traditional manner, the Kasavu border aligned precisely at the edge. Each piece is inspected before receiving the Handloom Mark, the certification that guarantees it was made on a handloom, by hand.

"The cloth holds the light differently than any mill can make."

A handloom saree from Eravathody is not a product. It is a record of the days of a weaver's life, the days it took to thread the loom, the mornings at the shuttle, the afternoons finishing the border.
